Rudy Gobert, C, Utah

Gobert is the most underrated player in the league right now. The best player on what has been the league’s best team this season, he’s a top-five MVP candidate by any objective measure. I remain stunned this isn’t more obvious.

Why? Utah has been awesome this season, but the Jazz still aren’t a national TV mainstay and their games are on late on the East Coast. Additionally, people have trouble understanding that Gobert is Utah’s best player, let alone somebody who should be an automatic top five in MVP ballots. Gobert rarely has the ball, and it’s harder to understand a guy away from the play might be the most important player.

As a result, a lot of people have the superficial impression that Donovan Mitchell — the Jazz’s highest-usage player — is the one carrying the team. TV broadcasts that spend two-thirds of the game talking up Mitchell aren’t helping. Mitchell is a star, don’t get me wrong, but he arguably hasn’t even been Utah’s best guard this year (Mike Conley has been tremendous; Mitchell, uncharacteristically bricky).

Meanwhile, the whole team is built around Gobert at both ends of the floor, to the point that the Jazz are massively worse when he’s off the court even though they have one of the league’s best backup centers and two legit Sixth Man of the Year candidates. (Utah outscores opponents by 15.4 points per 100 with Gobert on the floor … and are outscored by 1.5 with him off the court.)

His value is more apparent at the defensive end, where Gobert is likely to win Defensive Player of the Year, but observers undersell the extent of Gobert’s offensive impact. His only weakness is that he lacks the post game to dominate against switches, but he’s an elite screener with a huge catch radius around the rim and one with an unmatched motor for screening, re-screening and trucking down the lane every time. He shoots a league-leading 67.9 percent and draws heaps of fouls, but the real problem is on plays where he never gets the ball at all. To do that, defenses have to suck in so hard on his lob threat to prevent an automatic two points that it leaves Utah’s bevy of 3-point weapons open.

Overall, that’s added up to a much more dominant player than most people think. Three alphabet soup metrics have him rated second behind only Jokic this season (RAPTOR , EPM and LEBRON) in terms of his impact on a per-minute basis. Plus, as with Jokic, Gobert also has a massive durability advantage on most of his MVP-pursuing peers by playing 64 of Utah’s 65 games thus far.

Jokic will be the MVP, and I have no argument with that. I just don’t understand why Gobert isn’t getting more traction for the second spot on the ballot.
C'est de John Hollinger un des meilleurs analyste NBA.

Gobert c'est top 5 MVP, et puis c'est tout
